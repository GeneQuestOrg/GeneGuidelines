# Why we built this — the family story

A dentist noticed a suspicious mass in my ten-year-old son's jaw.

The initial histopathology, done at one of the largest hospitals in the country, came back with a diagnosis of **juvenile trabecular ossifying fibroma** — a rare, aggressive bone tumour whose standard treatment is surgical resection. Because the mass stretched from his teeth all the way up into his orbital floor, "resection" meant cutting away half of his face and putting the eye at risk.

Unconvinced, my wife — with a molecular biotechnology background — and I ordered a private genetic test on our own. The result came back as a **GNAS c.601C>T** mutation. That changes the diagnosis to **fibrous dysplasia**, and the international consensus for fibrous dysplasia in children is strict: **observation, not surgery**.

A senior facial surgeon in a major German city, knowing the corrected diagnosis, still offered to perform the operation at his clinic. He is one of the best in his field. But no surgeon, however skilled, can keep up with seven thousand rare genetic diseases — and the international FD/MAS consensus governing this specific entity is younger than his career and sits in one paper among hundreds his sub-field touches.

After the genetic result came back, a senior researcher from the 2019 international FD/MAS consensus working group re-examined the biopsy *pro bono*. That is the consortium model GeneGuidelines exists to scale.

Twice, my son was inches away from a life-altering surgery he didn't need.

**No child should wait for the right diagnosis because they didn't have a programmer for a parent.**

## What this means for the product

The diagnostic gap in rare disease is not a competence problem. It is a structural one — clinical experience in a rare entity is concentrated in a narrow set of centres, and finding them takes active effort that should not depend on luck.

GeneGuidelines is built around three convictions that follow directly from this story:

1. **Every recommendation needs a citation.** Every paragraph in every disease guideline is anchored to a specific PubMed ID, so a clinician (or a parent) can read the source themselves.
2. **The guideline must keep moving.** A static PDF goes stale in months. Our living guideline is updated by an AI Watcher and reviewed by named specialists, who rate each proposed change *useful / not useful* — a signal that ranks it for the next clinician who reads it.
3. **The specialist directory matters as much as the document.** When you do need a doctor who has actually treated this disease, you should be able to find them in seconds, ranked by published evidence — not by which centre happened to be on your insurance list.
